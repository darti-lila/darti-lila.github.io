Pulse

Transcutanious IoT tracking of glucose and heartrate monitoring

Project Description

The Transcutaneous Vital Signs Monitoring Device is a non-invasive wearable health monitor designed to continuously track heart rate, blood oxygen saturation (SpO₂), and glucose levels using near-infrared spectroscopy, eliminating the need for painful finger-prick blood tests.

The Transcutaneous Vital Signs Monitoring Device is a comprehensive health tracking system capable of measuring three critical vital signs simultaneously through optical sensing technology. The device realizes this by using two MAX30102 biosensors connected to an ESP32 microcontroller that processes photoplethysmography (PPG) signals in real-time. The beauty of this device is that it's completely non-invasive—users simply place their finger on the sensor, and the 880nm near-infrared light penetrates the skin to detect changes in blood flow, oxygen saturation, and glucose levels without any needles. The device has both local display via an OLED screen and cloud connectivity through a custom web dashboard called "VitalView" that logs historical data, displays live graphs, and provides weekly health summaries that can be shared with healthcare providers.

Key Features:

  • Dual MAX30102 sensor configuration for enhanced accuracy and data redundancy
  • Real-time monitoring of heart rate (BPM), blood oxygen saturation (SpO₂%), and glucose levels (mg/dL)
  • ESP32-powered with WiFi connectivity for cloud data synchronization
  • Custom web dashboard with patient information input, live vital signs display, and trend analysis
  • Portable battery-powered design with custom PCB for compact form factor

This was a 10-week freshman engineering design project I completed with my team (Ashley Benjamin, Earli Ismaili, Hakan Alex Kucukhuseyin, and Esila Özdemir) at Drexel University during Spring 2025. The biggest challenge was getting both sensors to work simultaneously without interference—we had to completely redesign our circuit and rewrite the code in Week 7 when we discovered accuracy issues. My role focused on circuit design, PCB layout (which we actually manufactured!), and programming the glucose monitoring algorithm in the final week. The project addresses a real problem: according to MIT research, more than half of diabetic patients don't test their glucose often enough because of the pain and inconvenience of needle pricks.

NOTE: This is a proof-of-concept prototype built for educational purposes. The glucose monitoring feature uses indirect correlation methods based on NIR spectroscopy and would require extensive clinical validation before medical use. The device showed promising results for heart rate and SpO₂ monitoring (comparable to commercial devices), but glucose readings are experimental. Future improvements include FDA certification pathways, medical-grade enclosure design, enhanced glucose detection algorithms with larger datasets, and extended battery life optimization. The device has potential applications in diabetes management, elderly care monitoring, and telemedicine remote patient monitoring systems!
